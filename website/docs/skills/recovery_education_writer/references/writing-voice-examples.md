# Writing Voice Examples: Annotated

This document provides annotated examples of the recovery education writing voice, showing what works and why.

---

## Example 1: Meth-Induced Paranoia

### Full Article Example

**Hook: Personal Vulnerability**
> I was convinced my neighbors were recording me through the walls. Not metaphorically—I could *hear* the clicks. The whir of the recording equipment. The shuffle of feet as they repositioned themselves to get a better angle. I knew, with absolute certainty, that they were building a case against me.
>
> It wasn't paranoia in the psychological sense. It was pattern recognition. My brain was doing its job—detecting threats, keeping me safe. Except the threats weren't real, and my brain's threat detector was running on a dopamine flood 12 times higher than baseline.

**Why this works:**
- ✅ Immediate vulnerability ("I was convinced...")
- ✅ Specific sensory details (clicks, whir, shuffle)
- ✅ Shows the internal logic ("It wasn't paranoia...it was pattern recognition")
- ✅ Bridges to science ("dopamine flood 12 times higher")
- ✅ No shame or judgment—just describing the experience

---

**Science: What's Literally Happening**
> Here's what's actually happening in your brain when meth causes paranoia:
>
> Methamphetamine floods your brain with dopamine—up to 1200% of baseline levels. That's not just "a lot." That's a biological tidal wave. Cocaine, by comparison, increases dopamine to about 350%. Food? Maybe 200% if it's really good pizza.
>
> But dopamine doesn't just make you feel good. It activates your salience network—the pattern-detection system that evolved to keep you alive by noticing threats before they kill you. Rustling in the bushes? THREAT. Shadow moving? THREAT. Click in the wall? THREAT.
>
> Your salience network is supposed to filter out irrelevant stimuli (background noise, shadows, coincidences) and highlight genuine threats (predators, danger, opportunities). But when you flood it with 12x normal dopamine, it loses the ability to distinguish signal from noise. Everything becomes significant. Every click is evidence. Every glance from a stranger is surveillance. Your brain is doing exactly what it evolved to do—detect patterns and protect you from threats. It's just doing it on overdrive, with a broken filter.

**Why this works:**
- ✅ Concrete numbers that are striking (1200%, not just "a lot")
- ✅ Comparison to other substances (cocaine, food) for context
- ✅ Explains normal function first ("pattern-detection system that evolved to keep you alive")
- ✅ Bridges mechanism to lived experience ("Click in the wall? THREAT.")
- ✅ Avoids jargon ("salience network" is explained immediately)
- ✅ Normalizes the response ("Your brain is doing exactly what it evolved to do")

---

**Timeline: When Does It Get Better?**
> The good news: Meth-induced paranoia is almost always temporary.
>
> **Acute psychosis** (hallucinations, severe paranoia): Typically resolves within **3-7 days** after stopping use, especially if you can sleep. Sleep deprivation makes psychosis exponentially worse—your brain needs REM sleep to reset.
>
> **Paranoid thoughts** (feeling watched, suspicious): Usually resolve within **2-4 weeks** as dopamine receptors start downregulating and your salience network recalibrates.
>
> **Full resolution**: Most people report complete resolution within **1-3 months**. Your brain's pattern detector returns to baseline.
>
> **Red flag**: If paranoia persists beyond 30 days after stopping meth, it may have triggered an underlying psychotic disorder (this is rare but possible). See a psychiatrist—antipsychotic medication can help.

**Why this works:**
- ✅ Specific timelines (3-7 days, 2-4 weeks, 1-3 months)
- ✅ Clarifies different phases (acute vs. lingering thoughts)
- ✅ Includes actionable info (sleep helps)
- ✅ Provides red flag threshold (30 days)
- ✅ Hope without false promises ("almost always temporary" not "always")

---

**Normalize: You're Not Broken**
> This isn't a character flaw, and it doesn't mean you're "crazy." It's a neurological response to a drug that hijacks the systems designed to keep you alive. Your brain isn't broken—it's responding exactly as it's wired to when dopamine levels are 12x normal.
>
> The fact that you're reading this, trying to understand what happened, is a sign that your rational brain is still working. That's the prefrontal cortex fighting back, trying to make sense of the salience network's false alarms. That's a good sign.

**Why this works:**
- ✅ Explicitly counters shame ("not a character flaw")
- ✅ Reframes "crazy" as neurological response
- ✅ Validates the reader's effort to understand
- ✅ Offers a small piece of hope ("your rational brain is still working")

---

**Action: What to Do Right Now**
> **If you're currently experiencing meth-induced paranoia:**
>
> 1. **Sleep.** Even a few hours helps. Paranoia gets exponentially worse with sleep deprivation. If you can't sleep, try to rest in a dark, quiet room.
>
> 2. **Ground yourself.** Name 5 things you can see, 4 you can hear, 3 you can touch, 2 you can smell, 1 you can taste. This interrupts the salience network's threat loop by anchoring you in the present.
>
> 3. **Tell someone you trust.** Paranoia thrives in isolation. Speaking your fears aloud often deflates them. "I think my neighbors are recording me" said out loud can help your prefrontal cortex reality-check the thought.
>
> 4. **Hydrate and eat.** Dehydration and low blood sugar worsen psychosis. Even if you don't feel hungry, try to eat something.
>
> 5. **Avoid redosing.** More meth = more dopamine = more paranoia. If you're trying to "come down," meth won't help—it'll make it worse.
>
> 6. **Seek medical help if:**
>    - Paranoia is severe (you're at risk of harming yourself or others)
>    - It's been >48 hours and it's not improving
>    - You're hallucinating (seeing/hearing things that aren't there)
>    - Emergency rooms can provide antipsychotics (Haldol, Zyprexa) that quickly reduce psychosis. You don't have to tell them you used meth—"I'm experiencing severe paranoia" is enough.
>
> **If you're in early recovery and paranoia is lingering:**
>
> 1. **Give it time.** If it's been &lt;30 days since you stopped using, your brain is still recalibrating. Most people notice significant improvement by week 3-4.
>
> 2. **See a psychiatrist.** If paranoia persists beyond 30 days, you may need short-term antipsychotic medication. This doesn't mean you're "crazy"—it means meth temporarily disrupted your dopamine system more than usual.
>
> 3. **Join a support community.** Hearing "I had that too" from others in recovery is incredibly normalizing. NA, SMART Recovery, or online forums (Reddit r/stopspeeding) can help.

**Why this works:**
- ✅ Tiered actions (immediate, short-term, long-term)
- ✅ Specific, actionable steps (not "take care of yourself")
- ✅ Includes harm reduction info for people still using ("avoid redosing")
- ✅ Destigmatizes seeking medical help
- ✅ Provides clear thresholds for when to escalate
- ✅ Offers community resources

## Example 2: The 6-Week Wall (Anhedonia)

**Hook: Personal Vulnerability**
> Around week six sober, I felt nothing. Not sad, not happy—just flat. I watched a sunset that would've made me cry a year ago and felt... nothing. My friend told a joke and I fake-laughed because I knew I was supposed to. Food tasted like cardboard. Music was just noise. I started to think, "If this is sobriety, what's the point?"
>
> I didn't know I was experiencing textbook anhedonia—the neurological inability to feel pleasure. I thought my brain was permanently broken. I thought I'd burned out my reward system for good. I was wrong.

**Why this works:**
- ✅ Visceral details (sunset, fake laugh, food like cardboard)
- ✅ The dangerous thought ("what's the point?")
- ✅ Reveals the fear ("permanently broken")
- ✅ Immediate reassurance ("I was wrong")
- ✅ Names the phenomenon (anhedonia) with context

---

**Science: What's Literally Happening**
> Anhedonia—the inability to feel pleasure—is one of the most common and least talked about symptoms of early recovery. Here's the neuroscience:
>
> When you were using (meth, cocaine, opioids, alcohol), your brain was flooded with artificial dopamine or endorphins—way more than any natural reward (food, sex, friendship) could provide. Over time, your brain adapted by downregulating receptors. It's like turning down the volume knob to protect against the constant blast.
>
> Now you're sober. The artificial flood is gone. But your receptors are still downregulated—the volume knob is still turned way down. So when you eat pizza or see a sunset or hear a joke, your brain gets a normal hit of dopamine... but there aren't enough receptors to respond. The signal is there, but your brain can't feel it.
>
> **This is not permanent.** Dopamine receptors upregulate over time—they turn the volume knob back up. But it's slow. Research shows it takes **6-12 months** for full receptor recovery after stimulant use, and **12-18 months** after long-term opioid use.

**Why this works:**
- ✅ Accessible metaphor (volume knob)
- ✅ Explains normal function first (brain adapting during use)
- ✅ Bridges to experience (pizza, sunset, joke)
- ✅ Explicit reassurance (NOT PERMANENT)
- ✅ Research-based timelines (6-12 months, 12-18 months)

---

**Timeline: When Does It Get Better?**
> Anhedonia follows a predictable (if brutal) timeline:
>
> **Weeks 1-3**: Often a "pink cloud" phase—relief at being sober, optimism, energy. This is adrenaline and novelty.
>
> **Weeks 4-8**: The wall. Anhedonia peaks. Nothing feels good. High relapse risk. This is when most people quit.
>
> **Months 3-4**: First glimpses of natural pleasure return. You'll have a "good hour" where music sounds good again, or food tastes like food. These moments are fleeting at first.
>
> **Months 4-6**: More good days than bad days. Dopamine receptors are upregulating. Natural rewards are starting to compete with the memory of the drug.
>
> **Months 6-12**: Emotional range fully restored. You can feel joy, excitement, contentment. Not every day is great, but you're capable of feeling good again.

**Why this works:**
- ✅ Month-by-month breakdown
- ✅ Acknowledges the hardest period (weeks 4-8)
- ✅ Describes what improvement feels like ("good hour")
- ✅ Sets realistic expectations (not linear improvement)
- ✅ Hope grounded in timeline

---

**Normalize: You're Not Broken**
> If you're in the anhedonia phase, you're not broken. You're exactly on schedule. This is your brain healing, even though it feels like dying.
>
> The fact that nothing feels good isn't a sign you're doing recovery wrong. It's a sign your dopamine receptors are still downregulated. And that's temporary.

**Why this works:**
- ✅ "Exactly on schedule" normalizes the experience
- ✅ Validates how hard it feels ("feels like dying")
- ✅ Reframes the symptom as healing

---

**Action: What to Do Right Now**
> **How to survive the 6-week wall:**
>
> 1. **Exercise.** This is the most evidence-based intervention. 30 minutes of cardio, 5x/week, has been shown to upregulate dopamine receptors faster than anything else. I know you don't want to. Do it anyway.
>
> 2. **Behavioral activation.** Schedule activities even if they don't feel good. See friends, go to meetings, play music, cook. You won't feel like it—that's the point. After 2-4 weeks of forcing yourself, natural motivation starts to return.
>
> 3. **Don't isolate.** Isolation makes anhedonia worse. Social connection activates reward pathways even if it doesn't feel good yet.
>
> 4. **Medication (if needed).** If anhedonia is unbearable and you're at high relapse risk, talk to a psychiatrist about Wellbutrin (bupropion). It supports dopamine function and can bridge you through the worst of it.
>
> 5. **Know the timeline.** Most people report significant improvement by month 3-4. You're not waiting forever—you're waiting 8-12 weeks.
>
> 6. **Call someone if you're thinking about relapsing.** The "what's the point?" thought is dangerous. Tell someone. Hearing "I felt that too, and it passed" can keep you alive.

**Why this works:**
- ✅ Evidence-based (#1 is exercise)
- ✅ Acknowledges resistance ("I know you don't want to")
- ✅ Explains why behavioral activation works
- ✅ Includes medical option without shame
- ✅ Repeats the timeline (8-12 weeks)
- ✅ Addresses relapse risk directly

## Example 3: Using Dreams

**Hook: Personal Vulnerability**
> I woke up with my heart pounding, frantically checking my arms for track marks that weren't there. In the dream, I'd shot up in a gas station bathroom—I could feel the needle, taste the drip, see the blood register in the syringe. It was so vivid I spent the first ten seconds of consciousness convinced I'd relapsed.
>
> Then the guilt hit. *Why did I dream about using? Does this mean I secretly want to? Am I failing at recovery?*
>
> No. It means my brain is healing.

**Why this works:**
- ✅ Visceral, immediate scene (checking arms)
- ✅ Sensory detail (feel, taste, see)
- ✅ The internal shame spiral (voiced as questions)
- ✅ Direct answer (No. It means...)
- ✅ Reframes as healing

---

**Science: What's Literally Happening**
> Using dreams—also called relapse dreams—are one of the most common and least understood phenomena in recovery. Research shows **70% of people in recovery** experience them, especially in the first 6 months.
>
> Here's the neuroscience:
>
> During sleep, your brain consolidates memories and prunes unused neural pathways. For months or years, "using" was a superhighway in your brain—a six-lane freeway of associations, muscle memory, and emotional triggers. Now that you're sober, your brain is trying to prune that highway down to a dirt road.
>
> But pruning takes time. And during REM sleep (dream sleep), your brain replays those pathways as it decides what to keep and what to let go. It's like your brain is saying, "Hey, we used to do this all the time. Should we still remember this?" The dream is your brain processing the memory—not creating a desire to repeat it.
>
> **Using dreams are not:**
> - A sign you secretly want to use
> - A sign you're failing at recovery
> - A premonition that you'll relapse
>
> **Using dreams are:**
> - A sign your brain is rewiring
> - A completely normal part of recovery
> - Often triggered by stress, anniversaries, or seeing cues (people, places)

**Why this works:**
- ✅ Normalizes with research (70%)
- ✅ Accessible metaphor (superhighway → dirt road)
- ✅ Explains mechanism (REM sleep consolidation)
- ✅ Explicit list of what they're NOT
- ✅ Reframes as healing

---

**Timeline: When Do They Stop?**
> Using dreams are most common in the **first 6 months** of recovery, but they can persist for years. Frequency and intensity typically decrease over time.
>
> **Common triggers:**
> - Stress (work, relationships, money)
> - Anniversaries (1 year sober, birthday, using anniversary)
> - Seeing cues (old friends, places you used, times of day)
> - Emotional overwhelm (grief, anger, loneliness)
>
> Some people report using dreams **years into recovery**. This doesn't mean you're doing recovery wrong—it means your brain encoded using as a significant life experience, and significant experiences get replayed during stress.

**Why this works:**
- ✅ Honest about timeline (can persist for years)
- ✅ Identifies triggers (stress, anniversaries)
- ✅ Normalizes long-term occurrence
- ✅ Removes shame ("doesn't mean you're doing recovery wrong")

---

**Normalize: You're Not Broken**
> The guilt you feel when you wake up from a using dream? That's actually a good sign. It means your values have shifted. The old you might've woken up from that dream with nostalgia or craving. The new you wakes up with guilt—because using isn't aligned with who you are anymore.
>
> The dream isn't a desire. It's your brain processing what was.

**Why this works:**
- ✅ Reframes guilt as progress
- ✅ Compares old self to new self (values shifted)
- ✅ Offers a new interpretation ("processing what was")

---

**Action: What to Do Right Now**
> **When you have a using dream:**
>
> 1. **Journal it immediately.** Writing it down externalizes it and reduces its emotional charge. You don't have to analyze it—just get it out of your head.
>
> 2. **Remind yourself: "Dreams aren't desires."** Say it out loud if you need to.
>
> 3. **Call/text your support person.** "Had a using dream, feeling shaky" is enough. You don't have to explain.
>
> 4. **Check for triggers.** Are you stressed? Is an anniversary coming up? Did you see someone/something that reminded you of using? Knowing the trigger helps you prepare for the next one.
>
> 5. **Expect them.** Using dreams are normal. If you go into recovery expecting them, they're less destabilizing when they happen.
>
> 6. **If they're frequent and disturbing:** Talk to a therapist. Trauma therapy (EMDR, IFS) can help process the emotional charge around using memories.

**Why this works:**
- ✅ Immediate action (journal)
- ✅ Cognitive reframe ("Dreams aren't desires")
- ✅ Social support (text someone)
- ✅ Investigates triggers
- ✅ Normalizes by expecting them
- ✅ Offers therapy for severe cases

## Voice Characteristics Across All Examples

### 1. Personal Pronouns (I/You/We)
- "I was convinced..." (not "Patients often report...")
- "Your brain is healing" (not "The brain heals...")
- "We're in this together" (peer, not clinician)

### 2. Concrete Details
- "Track marks," "gas station bathroom," "blood register"
- Not abstract: "intense dream experience"

### 3. Internal Dialogue
- "Does this mean I secretly want to?"
- Voices the shame spiral readers are experiencing

### 4. Explicit Reassurance
- "You're not broken"
- "This is temporary"
- Doesn't leave readers to infer hope

### 5. Accessible Metaphors
- "Volume knob"
- "Six-lane freeway → dirt road"
- Not: "Mesolimbic pathway pruning"

### 6. Specific Timelines
- "2-4 weeks"
- "6-12 months"
- Not vague: "it gets better eventually"

### 7. Action Over Explanation
- Always ends with "What to Do Right Now"
- Specific, feasible steps

### 8. Harm Reduction Integrated
- "If you're still using..."
- "If you're not ready to stop..."
- Doesn't exclude active users

## Common Voice Mistakes to Avoid

### ❌ Clinical Distance
> "Individuals with methamphetamine use disorder often experience paranoid ideation as a result of dopaminergic dysregulation."

### ✅ Peer Voice
> "Meth floods your brain with dopamine, which makes your pattern-detection system go haywire. That's why you hear clicks in the walls."

---

### ❌ Toxic Positivity
> "Just stay grateful! Focus on the positives!"

### ✅ Realistic Hope
> "This phase sucks. It's hard. But it's temporary, and there's a timeline."

---

### ❌ Scare Tactics
> "Meth will permanently destroy your brain. You'll never feel normal again."

### ✅ Science-Based Truth
> "Meth causes significant changes to dopamine receptors, but research shows they upregulate over 6-12 months. Your brain can heal."

---

### ❌ Moral Judgment
> "If you're serious about recovery, you'll push through the hard parts."

### ✅ Validation
> "If you relapsed during the 6-week wall, that's understandable. It's the hardest phase, and most people don't know it's coming."

---

## Key Takeaway

The voice should feel like a friend who's been there, who's nerdy about the science, and who wants you to survive. Not a clinician, not a cheerleader, not a preacher—just a peer with a flashlight in the dark.
